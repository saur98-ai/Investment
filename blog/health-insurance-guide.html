Why Your Company Health Insurance Is Not Enough

📅 May 2025 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ Akshay Maheshwari

Most salaried employees in India feel covered by their employer-provided group health insurance. The HR department mentions it during onboarding, it comes bundled in the offer letter, and it feels like a benefit worth having. And it is — until it isn't.

The problem is not that company health insurance is bad. The problem is that millions of working professionals treat it as their only health coverage, creating a dangerous gap that only becomes visible when they need it most.

The Three Critical Gaps in Company Health Insurance

Gap 1: It Ends When You Leave

Company group health insurance is tied entirely to your employment. The day you resign, get laid off, or retire, your coverage ends. If you are between jobs for even 30 days with no personal policy, you have zero coverage. If you develop a health condition during this gap, new insurers may treat it as a pre-existing condition — making future insurance expensive or difficult to obtain.

Gap 2: The Cover Is Often Too Low

The average company group health policy provides ₹3–5 Lakhs of coverage. While that sounded adequate a decade ago, today's hospital bills tell a different story:

Medical Event Approx. Cost (Private Hospital) Typical Company Cover
Appendix surgery₹80,000–₹1.5 LakhsUsually covered
Angioplasty (heart)₹2.5–₹4.5 LakhsPartially covered
Cancer treatment (1 year)₹8–₹25 LakhsSeverely underinsured
Kidney transplant₹5–₹10 LakhsOften exceeds cover
ICU stay (7 days)₹2–₹5 LakhsPartially covered

Medical inflation in India runs at 10–15% per year. A ₹3 Lakh cover today will be equivalent to barely ₹1.8 Lakh in real terms by 2030.

Gap 3: No Continuity or Portability

Company policies are group policies negotiated by your employer. You have no control over coverage terms, hospital network, sub-limits on specific treatments, or co-payment clauses. You cannot port the policy to a personal plan without fresh underwriting — and at a later age, that means higher premiums or exclusions.

"A health emergency does not wait for your next employer's HR to process your insurance forms. Your personal policy is the one that shows up when it matters."

What Coverage Do You Actually Need?

For a family of 4 (couple + 2 children) in a metro city, a ₹10 Lakh family floater policy is a sensible minimum. For families with older parents, ₹15–20 Lakhs is recommended. Here is a framework:

  • Self + spouse + children (under 25): ₹10 Lakh family floater
  • Self + spouse (no children): ₹10 Lakh family floater
  • Senior parents (separate policy): ₹5–10 Lakh each, or a dedicated senior citizen plan
  • Target total: ₹15–20 Lakhs of combined individual + floater coverage

Buy Young: The Premium Advantage Is Huge

Health insurance premiums are heavily age-linked. The same ₹10 Lakh cover that costs ₹8,000–12,000 per year for a 30-year-old can cost ₹25,000–40,000 at age 50. More importantly, health conditions that develop in your 40s — diabetes, hypertension, thyroid issues — become pre-existing conditions that attract loading charges or exclusions.

Buying a personal policy while you are young and healthy locks you in at a lower base premium. Once issued, insurers cannot cancel or radically re-price your policy as long as you renew without a break.

The Critical Illness Rider: Your Financial Parachute

A Critical Illness rider pays a lump sum on diagnosis of a covered condition — cancer, heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, and typically 30–50 other critical illnesses. This is fundamentally different from a regular health policy, which reimburses hospital bills.

The lump sum is crucial because serious illness creates income loss (you stop working), home care costs, and lifestyle adjustments that a hospitalisation-only policy never covers. Add this rider to your base health policy for a few thousand rupees more — it is well worth it.

Super Top-Up: The Budget-Friendly Solution

If budget is a concern, consider a super top-up policy. It activates only after your total annual claims exceed a threshold (called the deductible). For example, a ₹20 Lakh super top-up with a ₹5 Lakh deductible costs roughly ₹5,000–8,000 per year for a 35-year-old. Combined with your company's ₹5 Lakh cover acting as the deductible buffer, you now have effectively ₹25 Lakhs of total coverage at a fraction of the cost of a standalone ₹25 Lakh policy.

It is one of the most cost-efficient ways to dramatically improve your health coverage without paying full premiums on a high-sum policy.

Key Takeaway

Company health insurance ends with your job and typically covers only ₹3–5 Lakhs — inadequate given today's hospital bills. Buy a personal ₹10 Lakh+ family floater policy while young; premiums at 30 are 3x cheaper than at 50. Add a Critical Illness rider and consider a super top-up plan for affordable, comprehensive coverage.
