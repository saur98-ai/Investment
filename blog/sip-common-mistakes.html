7 SIP Mistakes That Are Silently Killing Your Returns

📅 May 2025 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ Akshay Maheshwari

Starting a SIP is one of the best financial decisions you can make. But starting a SIP and making it work are two different things. Many investors do the right thing in principle and then inadvertently undermine it through small, avoidable behaviours. Here are the seven most common mistakes — and how to fix them.

1 Stopping Your SIP When the Market Falls

This is the most damaging and most common mistake. When markets crash — and they will, every few years — the emotional response is to stop the SIP and "wait for stability." This is precisely backwards.

When markets fall, your fixed SIP amount buys more units at lower prices. A ₹10,000 SIP buying units at ₹100 gets 100 units. The same SIP during a crash at ₹60 gets 167 units. When the market recovers (and it always has), those extra units drive dramatically higher returns. This is the core mechanism of rupee cost averaging — and stopping the SIP during a fall destroys it entirely.

"The stock market is the only market where customers run out of the store when there is a sale."

The fix: treat your SIP like an EMI. It goes out automatically, regardless of headlines. If anything, a market fall is a signal to increase your SIP, not stop it.

2 Investing in Too Many Funds (Over-Diversification)

A common misconception is that more funds equals more diversification. In practice, most diversified equity mutual funds own 40–80 of the same large-cap stocks. Owning 12 different large-cap funds does not reduce your risk — it just multiplies your paperwork and creates a portfolio that is impossible to track meaningfully.

For most investors, 3–5 well-chosen funds across categories (one large-cap or index, one flexi-cap, one mid/small-cap, one debt fund for near-term goals) is more than sufficient. If you have more than 8 active SIPs, it is worth doing a consolidation review.

3 Investing Without a Specific Goal

A SIP without a goal is like driving without a destination. You might end up somewhere, but probably not where you wanted to be. Goal-less investors are also far more likely to stop or redeem their SIPs early — because without a purpose, any emotional market event becomes a reason to exit.

For every SIP you run, define: (a) what it is for, (b) when you need the money, and (c) how much you will need. This determines the right fund category, investment horizon, and monthly amount. It also gives you something to hold onto when markets get choppy.

4 Ignoring the Expense Ratio

The expense ratio is the annual fee charged by the fund house, deducted daily from your NAV. It seems tiny — the difference between a 0.5% direct plan and a 1.5% regular plan is "just 1%." But over 20 years, that 1% difference is enormous.

That 1% expense ratio difference costs you approximately ₹15–30 Lakhs over a 20-year SIP, depending on the corpus size. Always prefer direct plans where possible, or work with a fee-based advisor who does not take commissions.

5 Not Doing a Step-Up SIP

A flat ₹10,000 SIP started in 2005 felt significant then. By 2025, with inflation, the real value of that ₹10,000 has halved. If your SIP amount never grows, your effective investment is shrinking in real terms every year.

A 10% annual step-up aligns your investment growth with your salary growth. Starting at ₹5,000 at age 25 with 10% annual step-up generates approximately ₹10.8 Crore by age 60 at 12% — versus ₹3.5 Crore with a flat SIP. The step-up is not optional for serious wealth creation; it is essential.

6 Choosing the Wrong Fund for Your Goal

An equity SIP in a small-cap fund is excellent for a 15-year goal. It is terrible for a goal 2 years away. Equity markets can fall 40–50% in a bear market. If your daughter's college admission fee is due in 18 months and your SIP is in a mid-cap fund, a market downturn could force you to redeem at a loss.

Match fund risk to goal timeline:

  • Under 2 years: Liquid / ultra short-term debt funds
  • 2–5 years: Hybrid / balanced advantage funds
  • 5–10 years: Large-cap or flexi-cap equity funds
  • 10+ years: Mid-cap, small-cap, or multi-cap equity funds

7 Checking Your NAV Every Day

A SIP is a long-term instrument. Checking the NAV daily is like weighing yourself five times a day while on a diet — the short-term fluctuations are noise, and obsessing over them leads to bad decisions. Investors who check daily are far more likely to panic-sell during corrections or get overconfident during bull runs.

A quarterly review of your SIP portfolio — checking whether you are on track for your goal, whether funds are performing in line with their benchmarks, and whether your asset allocation needs rebalancing — is entirely sufficient. Let your SIP run. Trust the process. Check in every three months, not every three hours.

Key Takeaway

The biggest SIP mistakes are behavioural: stopping during market falls, over-diversifying, investing without goals, ignoring expense ratios (a 1% difference costs ~₹15-30 Lakhs over 20 years), skipping step-up SIPs, choosing wrong funds, and checking NAV obsessively. Fix these, and your returns will improve significantly without changing a single fund.
