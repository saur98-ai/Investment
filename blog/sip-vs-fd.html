SIP vs FD: The Real Numbers Nobody Shows You

📅 May 2025 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ Akshay Maheshwari

Every Indian family has a fixed deposit. It sits in the bank, earning a tidy 6–7%, and everyone sleeps soundly at night. FDs feel safe. They feel responsible. And for decades, they were the default answer to "where should I keep my savings?"

But here is the uncomfortable truth: once you account for tax and inflation, your FD might be giving you negative real returns. And while you sleep soundly, your money is quietly losing purchasing power.

The Tax Problem with FDs

FD interest is added to your income and taxed at your slab rate. If you are in the 30% tax bracket, a 7% FD actually earns you just 4.9% post-tax. Now factor in India's long-run average inflation of around 6%, and your real return is negative 1.1%.

That means every year, the money sitting in your FD is buying less than it did the year before. You feel richer in rupee terms, but you are actually poorer in real terms.

"Inflation is the silent tax that nobody voted for — and your FD is the one paying it."

The 20-Year Numbers Side by Side

Let's compare ₹10,000 invested every month for 20 years in two scenarios: a bank FD (6% post-tax, roughly) and an equity mutual fund SIP (12% long-run historical average).

  • FD at 6% for 20 years: ₹46.4 Lakhs (total invested: ₹24 Lakhs)
  • SIP at 12% for 20 years: ₹99.9 Lakhs — nearly ₹1 Crore (total invested: ₹24 Lakhs)

Same money. Same discipline. Same 20 years. But one gives you ₹46 Lakhs and the other gives you ₹1 Crore. That is a difference of over ₹53 Lakhs — just from choosing the right instrument.

The Myth That FD Is "Safer"

Here is the thing about safety: there are two kinds of risk. The risk of losing money in the short term (volatility), and the risk of not having enough money when you need it (shortfall risk). FDs protect you perfectly against the first. But they expose you directly to the second.

If you are saving for retirement 20 years away, the real danger is not a 15% market correction — it is arriving at retirement with ₹46 Lakhs when you needed ₹1 Crore. That is shortfall risk, and FDs carry it in spades.

What About ELSS for Tax Saving?

If you are still parking money in tax-saving FDs (5-year lock-in), consider switching to ELSS — Equity Linked Saving Schemes. Both qualify for Section 80C deduction up to ₹1.5 Lakhs. But ELSS has only a 3-year lock-in (shortest among 80C instruments) and historically delivers 12–15% returns over any 5-year period. Tax-saving FD returns hover around 6–7%. The math is decisive.

When Does FD Make Sense?

FDs are not useless. They serve a purpose — just not as a wealth-creation tool. Use FDs for:

  • Your emergency fund (3–6 months of expenses)
  • Short-term goals (1–2 years away)
  • Capital preservation in retirement (a portion of your corpus)
  • Senior citizens seeking regular income (with better tax treatment)

For everything else — goals 5+ years away, children's education, retirement planning, wealth building — SIPs in diversified equity mutual funds are the proven, superior choice.

The numbers don't lie. ₹10,000 per month, 20 years, 12%: that is ₹99.9 Lakhs. The only question is whether you start today or keep waiting.

Key Takeaway

FDs feel safe but deliver negative real returns after tax and inflation. A ₹10,000/month SIP over 20 years at 12% grows to ₹99.9 Lakhs — more than double the ₹46.4 Lakhs an FD would give. Use FDs for short-term safety; use SIPs for long-term wealth.
